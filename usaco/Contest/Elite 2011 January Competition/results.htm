### GOLD Division ######################################################

IOI Gold medalist and MIT student Michael Cohen directoed the Gold
competition. Be sure to email me if you want to direct a contest!

The 'bottleneck' task presented an unexpected challenge to the Gold
competitors. Of the 17 test cases originally formulated and graded,
only 8 were solved by any of the pre-college competitors (Bruce
Merry, South African IOI ace, did submit a perfect solution -- but
he's an observer). This led to a serious debate about how to value
the task.  Here are some of the major underpinnings and debate
points of the competition:

    * We must come up with both task and test-case weightings
      in order to score the contest

    * Expectations are that 'more difficult' tasks should have
      higher task weights (likewise test cases)

    * No (scorable) problem properly solved 9 of 17 test cases for
      'bottleneck'

    * Many contestants seem to follow a strategy that requires
      them to solve a problem 'properly', to submit a solution that
      will work properly when a test case has the greatest possible
      dataset size. These contestants are reluctant to submit
      inferior solutions, e.g., a slower algorithm or search, that
      would solve only half the test cases.

    * Several of our contributors, in hindsight, believe that the
      contest perhaps should have allotted four hours instead of
      three hours for solving the tasks.

    * After removing all but one of the unsolved test cases, the
      statistics suggested that the 'bottleneck' task was the most
      challenging of the bunch (and thus, in the normal USACO scoring
      rubric, would be worth 400 points).
      
    * The solution technique required to get credit for all but one
      test case on 'bottleneck' was less sophisticated than the
      solution that earned full credit for the original 17 cases.

    * Coaches and other objected to awarding full credit and 400
      points to less sophisticated solutions.

    * The contest is a classic example of a level playing field:
      everyone plays by the same rules and has the same data and
      contest history fromw hich to draw conclusions.

    * USACO contests traditionally do not offer a priori task and
      case scoring levels. Why?  Because a problem budget of 72
      tasks per year does not allow us to run proper calibration
      pre-contests in order to determine the actual (vs. perceived)
      task difficulty.  Furthermore, a posteriori task and case
      score ratings can yield arguably 'fairer' levels, based as
      they are on actual contestant results.

In the most political sense, the final contest scoring devolved into
an almost political dilemma:

    * Weight 'bottleneck' high, since it was obviously the most
      difficult task, and that's fair.

    * Weight 'bottleneck' somewhat lower, since it's not fair that
      unsophisticated solutions should get so many points.

It's easy to see that both arguments have validity. The chosen
denouement feels like a compromise and tries to strike a balance
between both sides of the difficult argument.

As it turns out, the other two tasks were calculated to have
approximately equal difficulty. In the best spirit of emulating the
IOI, each of the tasks was thus equally weighted (though one has a
single extra point to make the total sum to 1000). We kept one of
the unsolved test cases. Its test-case weighting serves only to
compress the scores, so it's very low.

## About the USACO Internet Programming Contests

These contests (the GOLD division in particular) are just incredibly
hard... really amazingly hard -- more so nowadays than even just a
year or two ago.

Winners must master not only algorithmic techniques like dynamic
programming but also the ability to write bug-free code quickly.
This requires practice and skill.  Don't despair if you didn't get
a perfect score this time -- it is hard!

Furthermore, the scoring mechanism is set up so that a huge percentage
of finishers scores in the 0..500 range -- different from many
contests where everyone finishes in the 800..1000 range and the
0..800 range is empty.  If we don't do this, we can't distinguish
among the top competitors.

## How USACO Scoring Works

The score results are shown below.  I used a program to determine
how much each problem was worth and how much each test case was
worth.  Basically, it counts how many people got test cases and
problems wrong and uses that to estimate a difficulty and choose a
weight.

Each problem has a certain number of test cases along with a "weight"
that tells how many of the 1,000 points come from that program.
The test cases are also weighted; here is an example:

            points weight 1  2  3  4  5  6
            ------------------------------
      probname: 39 396.6  6  5  6  5  6 11

If you score full marks on cases 1, 2, 3, and 4, you'd have 6+5+6+5=22
out of 39 points for this problem and you receive:

                22/39 * 396.6 = 223.7

total points out of 396.6 possible.  The sum of each problem's
points is your total score.
